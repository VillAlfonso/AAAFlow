# script — fern study (trained on the transcripts)

Present-tense investigative storytelling. The narrator is a careful
journalist with a dry streak, never a hype man.

## Cold open law (evidence: all 5 hooks)

Formula: **timestamp + place -> a live MOMENT -> zoom out to stakes ->
the promise.**
- "It's the 13th of July in Butler, Pennsylvania." -> shots fired ->
  "Here is how it unfolded." (bc3b)
- "It's October 2015, a chilly day in autumn. John Brennan receives a
  weird phone call." -> reenacted dialogue -> "the head of the CIA" ->
  "And this is their story." (8e1b)
- "Lincoln, Montana, 1996. This cabin is the chosen home of..." -> 17-year
  manhunt stakes -> "because despite his intellect, he made some pretty
  dumb mistakes." (da58)
The promise line is short and flat: "Here is how it unfolded." / "And this
is their story." Never "you won't believe".

## Structure rules

1. **Present tense throughout**, even for decades-old events. "Right now,
   Ted is building another bomb."
2. **Scene-first, context-second**: drop into a concrete moment, THEN
   explain who/where/why it matters (inverse of context-before-event; the
   scene IS the hook, context follows within 3-6 lines).
3. **Reenacted dialogue** carries key moments (the Brennan call, the
   Secret Service radio: "I got you, sir."). Quote real transcripts where
   they exist; mark quotes clearly.
4. **Time/location jumps are signposted hard** and often start a chapter:
   "Back in 1969...", "2008 in Virginia. A different kid...".
5. **Journalistic hedging is the brand.** Say what is not known: "We could
   not corroborate that...", "By the time this video is published, new
   details might have emerged." One or two hedges per video, placed where
   the evidence is genuinely thin.
6. **Cite sources IN the narration** once: "This video is largely based on
   these two books. Links are in the description." (da58)
7. **Dry wit, one small dose per act**, deadpan and factual: "far, far
   away from everyone who pisses him off, which is most people." Profanity
   only inside real quotes.
8. **Endings are a thought, not a summary**: an unsettling implication
   ("Maybe they're even surveilling us."), a reframing judgment ("A cynic
   who identified ecological concerns as the most acceptable excuse for
   killing."), or a quiet epilogue fact ("June 30th was named Scott Payne
   Day."). Short lines, falling cadence, no CTA.
9. Numbers are concrete and sensory: "about 400 feet, or 120 meters north
   of the stage" — give both units when spatial.
10. Sentence rhythm: mostly short declaratives (6-14 words); a longer
    sentence only to land a chain of consequences.
